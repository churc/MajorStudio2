Who works in - and who visits - museums?

Using publicly available datasets from the National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) audience data, reported findings from two national art museum staff demographic surveys, and New York City Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) grantee staff survey dataset, together with artwork data from Brooklyn Museum, these visualizations look at staffing, visitors, and acquisitions.

 

Local museum staff: 2015

at New York City Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) funded, larger budget museums.
A grantee staff survey, developed out of a similar effort to measure the diversity of staff working in US museums conducted the previous year, was commissioned in 2015.

7,346 paid museum staff, plus board members, work at NYC museums with budgets larger than $1 million. The Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) survey identified staff by 22 job types and grouped by 2010 U.S. Census race and ethnicity categories. Museum staff numbers by race and ethnicity can be seen along the horizontal axis - each yellow circle shows the number for each job type. Numbers tell one part of the story, by clicking the percent button, percentages horizontally by race and ethnicity can be seen.

National Art Audiences

1982-2017 show changing art museum audience numbers. National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) data reports that 21%-26.7% of U.S. adults visited an art museum or gallery in any given year.

who goes to art museums? 2015: NEA noted about 79% of audiences were white non-Hispanic, 9% Hispanic, and 6% were African-American, despite respectively being about 69%, 14%, and 11% of the U.S. population at that time.

trend lines for race and ethnicity for years 2012 - 2017 detail variation in NEA-reported attendance increases

The 2000 U.S. Census groupings have been used to categorize employees and audiences by race and Hispanic/Latino status. As the DCLA 2015 survey report rightly pointed out, these separate categories are inadequate, since many individuals do not identify with these groups. However, they are the Census categories at present so are cautiously used here.

National art museum staff: 2015-2018

Two Art Museum Staff Demographic Surveys, 2015 and 2018, were also undertaken by Ithaka S+R, with the assistance of an advisory committee of Association of Art Museum Directors (AAMD) staff and member museum leaders. Nationally in 2015, staff were about ten percentage points more racially and ethnically homogenous than the U.S. population.

trends 2015-2018 were towards diversity. New staff hires showed a decrease of white non-Hispanic employees from 74% to 65%.
A 2018 comparison of "intellectual leadership" new hiring though shows a difference between curatorial and education positions as compared to art museum leadership, including executive positions, and conservation staff - leadership and conservation are behind the trend.

To conclude

Who are museums for? Looking at representational diversity here is just a part of the conversation about museums, and museums are not only artworks, staff, and audiences, but also a myriad of intersecting parts: buildings, budgets, history, culture, exhibitions, to name just a few. Art museums are places that collect, curate, and exhibit works of art, and in doing so, interpret and shape our culture.
Availability of open data here limits possible findings to inferences; related sets of information indicate stories. People are writing about art institutions and rethinking what they can be. What do those who are not at present included in art museums and do not visit envisage in order for museums to be places for everyone?
