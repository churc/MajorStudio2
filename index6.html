There are 2,620 art museums across the U.S. Who works in - and who visits - museums? Using publicly available datasets from the National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) audience data, reported findings from two national art museum staff demographic surveys,

and New York City Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) grantee staff survey dataset, together with artwork data from Brooklyn Museum, these visualizations look at staffing, visitors, and acquisitions.

Local museum staff: 2015

at New York City-funded, larger budget museums. A grantee staff survey, developed out of a similar effort to measure the diversity of staff in U.S. art museums, was commissioned in 2015.

7,346 paid museum staff, plus board members, work at NYC museums with budgets larger than $1 million. The 2015 Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) survey identified staff by 22 job types and grouped by 2010 U.S. Census race and ethnicity categories. Museum staff numbers by race and ethnicity can be seen along the horizontal axis - each yellow circle shows the number for each job type.
Numbers tell one part of the story, by clicking the percent button, percentages horizontally by race and ethnicity can be seen. 38.36% of paid museum staff and board members at these larger museums (2,817 people) identify as people of color - compared to 66.7% of the NYC population at that time. The findings from Ithaka S+R and funding partner, The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, had a big impact on the city’s cultural community. Following the report, the DCLA

initiated a cultural plan, reaching out to hear priorities, concerns, and ideas across the boroughs from nearly 200,000 local residents, and July 2017, released CreateNYC Plan. One of the unusual actions was to tie funding to diversity plans, “DCLA will introduce new diversity measures for our city's cultural organizations including collecting data from grantees about the diversity of staff and board members; asking for organizations' approaches to Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI); and in FY 2019 will require DEI plans from the CIG" - the Cultural Institutions Group, 33 members whose institutions are on city-owned property (including The Metropolitan Museum of Art, and Brooklyn Museum.) These institutions operate as publicly-owned facilities whose mandate is to provide cultural services accessible to all New Yorkers.

National Art Audiences

1982-2017 shows changing art museum audience numbers. National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) data reports that 21% - 26.7% of U.S. adults visited an art museum or gallery that year.

Who goes to art museums? In 2015 NEA reported about 79%
of audiences were white non-Hispanic, 9% Hispanic, and 6% were African-American, despite respectively being about 69%, 14%, and 11% of the U.S. population.

National art museum staff: 2015-2018

Two Art Museum Staff Demographic Surveys in 2015 and 2018 were also undertaken by Ithaka S+R, with the assistance from an advisory committee of Association of Art Museum Directors (AAMD) staff and member museum leaders. In 2015, art museum staff were found to be about ten percentage points more racially and ethnically homogenous than the U.S. population.

From 2015-2018 trends were towards diversity with new staff hires for repeat participants showing an increase of people of color from 26% to 35%. However, "intellectual leadership" hiring revealed a difference between curatorial and education positions when compared to art museum leadership, including executive positions, and conservation staff. Leadership and conservation were found to be behind the trend.

To conclude

Who are museums for? Looking at representational diversity here is just a part of the conversation about museums, and museums are not only artworks, staff, and audiences, but also a myriad of intersecting parts: buildings, budgets, history, culture, and exhibitions. Art museums are institutions that collect, curate, and exhibit works of art, and in doing so contribute to interpreting and shaping our pluralistic culture.
Availability of open data here limits possible findings to inferences; these related sets of information

indicate stories. People are writing about art institutions and rethinking what they can be. How do those who are not included in museums envisage for art museums?
