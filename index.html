There are 2,620 art museums across the U.S. Who works in - and who visits - museums? Employing publicly available datasets from the National Endowment for the Arts (NEA), and the New York City Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) grantee staff

survey, and findings from two national art museum staff demographic surveys, as well as artwork data from the Brooklyn Museum, these visualizations examine staffing, visitors, and acquisitions.

Local museum staff: 2015

at New York City-funded, larger budget museums. A grantee staff survey undertaken by Ithaka S&R, developed out of a similar effort of theirs to measure the diversity of staff in U.S. art museums, was commissioned in 2015.

7,346 paid museum staff, including 802 board members (paid and volunteer), work at NYC museums with budgets of $1 million or more. The 2015 Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) survey identified staff by 22 job types and grouped by 2010 US Census race and ethnicity categories. Museum staff numbers are sorted by race and ethnicity along a horizontal axis. Hovering over each yellow circle reveals how many people work in this job category.
Numbers tell one part of the story. The pink percent button tracks a single ethnic or racial group along a horizontal axis. Hovering over a pink circle reveals what percentage of that racial group works in that specific job category, e.g. Facilities or Education. The green button sorts the percentages by job type along the vertical axis, showing the percentage of each racial and ethnic group for each job category.
In 2015, 38.36% of paid staff and board members at these museums (2,817 people) identify as people of color - compared to 66.7% of the NYC population.

This chart illustrates the museum 'discipline' within the larger DCLA cultural survey. The 2015 findings from Ithaka S+R and its funding partner, The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, had a big impact on the city’s cultural community. Following the report, the DCLA initiated a cultural plan, reaching out to nearly 200,000 local residents. In July 2017, the CreateNYC Plan was released. One aspect of this plan involved tying funding to diversity plans: “DCLA will introduce new diversity measures for our city's cultural organizations including collecting data from grantees about the diversity of staff and board members; asking for organizations' approaches to Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI); and in FY 2019 will require DEI plans from the CIG [Cultural Institutions Group]." The CIG is a group of 33 institutions on city-owned property (including The Metropolitan Museum of Art, and Brooklyn Museum.) These institutions operate as publicly-owned facilities whose mandate is to provide cultural services accessible to all New Yorkers.

National Art Audiences

1982-2017 shows changing art museum audience attendance numbers. National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) data reports that 21% - 26.7% of U.S. adults visited an art museum or gallery that year.

Who goes to art museums? In 2015, the NEA reported that about 79% of audiences were white non-Hispanic, 9% were Hispanic, and 6% were African-American. These groups constitute approximately 69%, 14%, and 11% of the U.S. population.

Trend lines for years 2012 - 2017 show variations in attendance for various race and ethnic subgroups.

The 2000 US Census groupings have been used to categorize employees and audiences by race and Hispanic/Latino status. As the DCLA 2015 survey report pointed out, these separate categories

are inadequate, since many individuals do not identify with these groups. However, they are the Census categories at present so are - cautiously - used here.

National art museum staff: 2015-2018

Two Art Museum Staff Surveys were also undertaken by Ithaka S+R, with an advisory committee of Association of Art Museum Directors staff and member museums. In 2015, art museum staff were found to be about ten percentage points more racially and ethnically homogenous than the U.S. population.

From 2015-2018 trends were towards diversity with new staff hires for repeat participants showing an increase of people of color from 26% to 35%. However, "intellectual leadership" hiring revealed a difference between curatorial and education positions when compared to art museum leadership, including executive positions, and conservation staff. Leadership and conservation were found to be behind the trend.

Who are museums for? Looking at representational diversity here is just a part of the conversation about museums, and museums are not only artworks, staff, and audiences, but also a myriad of intersecting parts: buildings, budgets, history, culture, and exhibitions. Art museums are institutions that collect, curate, and exhibit works of art, and in doing so contribute to interpreting and shaping our pluralistic culture.
Availability of open data here limits possible findings to inferences; these related sets of information

indicate stories. People are writing about art institutions and rethinking what they can be. How do those who are not included in museums envisage for art museums?
